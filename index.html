Introduction to Platform Security

Platform Security is a comprehensive approach to safeguarding computing platforms—including hardware, operating systems, software applications, and data—from internal and external threats. It plays a vital role in ensuring that systems remain secure, reliable, and functional while maintaining the trust of users and organizations. The lesson emphasizes the importance of securing all layers of a platform: physical security protects hardware from theft or tampering; firmware and boot security prevent unauthorized system startups; operating system security enforces updates and access control; application security guards against malicious code; network security uses firewalls and VPNs to protect data in transit; and data security employs encryption to keep sensitive information safe.

The CIA Triad—Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability—serves as the core foundation of information security. Confidentiality ensures that private data is accessed only by authorized individuals, integrity guarantees that information remains accurate and unaltered, and availability ensures that authorized users can access systems and data when needed. To strengthen these principles, authentication methods such as passwords, tokens, and biometrics verify user identities, while multi-factor authentication (MFA) adds an extra layer of protection. The lesson also covers non-repudiation, which ensures accountability by preventing users from denying their actions, and privacy, which protects individuals’ personal data in compliance with laws such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).

Furthermore, the lesson discusses Information Security Risk Management, a structured process that involves identifying valuable assets, recognizing vulnerabilities, and analyzing threats that could exploit them. Threat actors can include insiders, hackers, competitors, or automated bots. Once risks are assessed, organizations apply treatment strategies such as avoidance (eliminating the risk), mitigation (reducing the impact), acceptance (acknowledging the risk), or transference (shifting it to another party, like insurance).

In summary, platform security integrates technological measures, organizational policies, and human awareness to create a multi-layered defense against cyberattacks. By applying the CIA Triad, enforcing authentication, ensuring privacy, and managing risks effectively, organizations can maintain the stability, confidentiality, and resilience of their information systems in an increasingly digital world.

Security Awareness Training

The Security Awareness Training lesson focuses on the vital role humans play in cybersecurity. It explains that people are often the weakest link in an organization’s defense, as even advanced security tools cannot prevent errors caused by carelessness or lack of awareness. The lesson cites the PhilHealth Medusa attack as an example of how data breaches can lead to serious consequences such as identity theft, fraud, and claim issues. It reminds individuals that everyone is a potential target because attackers seek access to company systems through weak passwords, reused credentials, and unsecured personal devices.

A major topic discussed is phishing attacks, one of the most common forms of cybercrime. Phishing can occur through emails (email phishing), text messages (smishing), phone calls (vishing), and fake websites that trick users into revealing personal or financial information. The lesson teaches how to identify phishing attempts by recognizing warning signs such as unknown senders, unrealistic promises, poor grammar, suspicious links, and messages with a sense of urgency. To protect themselves, individuals are encouraged to verify the sender’s identity, avoid clicking on unfamiliar links, never share personal information, and use official websites instead of links provided in messages.

The lesson also covers malware, software created by cybercriminals to harm or exploit devices and networks. It lists indicators of infection such as slow performance, frequent pop-ups, and device overheating. Other threats include business email compromise, where hackers impersonate trusted contacts to steal money or data, and insider threats, where employees misuse their access to systems for revenge or personal gain. To counter these risks, the training advises enabling two-factor authentication (2FA), avoiding suspicious downloads, and verifying unusual requests through alternative communication channels.

Lastly, the lesson provides essential cyber hygiene practices to enhance online safety. These include performing regular data backups to recover from attacks, keeping software and systems up to date to patch vulnerabilities, and using strong, unique passphrases instead of simple passwords. It also promotes the use of password managers for secure storage. The lesson concludes by emphasizing that data is the new gold, meaning that personal and organizational information holds immense value. Therefore, everyone must take responsibility for protecting their data and maintaining privacy to ensure a safer and more secure digital environment.

Platform Architecture

The Platform Architecture lesson explains the structure and layers of a computing platform, which include hardware, firmware, operating systems, and applications. Hardware consists of physical components such as the CPU, memory, and storage, while firmware like BIOS or UEFI initializes the hardware before the system starts. The operating system (OS) manages hardware resources and enforces security, and applications provide user-level services. The lesson also compares mobile and desktop platforms, noting that mobile platforms focus on portability, battery efficiency, and app sandboxing, while desktops offer higher performance and flexibility but have a larger attack surface. Additionally, it discusses cloud and virtualization platforms, which increase resource efficiency but introduce new risks such as misconfigurations and VM escape attacks.

A key topic in the lesson is the Trusted Platform Module (TPM), a hardware-based security chip that acts as the Root of Trust in a computing platform. TPM operates independently from the main CPU and provides secure storage, encryption, and attestation. It ensures secure booting by verifying that the system has not been tampered with before starting the operating system, protecting against firmware and boot-level malware. The lesson also introduces UEFI, the modern replacement for BIOS, which serves as a bridge between hardware and the OS. Together with TPM, it supports the Chain of Trust, where each stage of the boot process verifies the integrity of the next stage. The lesson also differentiates TPM 1.2 and TPM 2.0, highlighting that TPM 2.0 offers stronger cryptographic algorithms and is required for modern systems such as Windows 11.

The lesson then explores Virtualization, a technology that allows multiple virtual machines (VMs) to run simultaneously on one physical server. Each VM operates independently with its own OS and applications, optimizing hardware utilization and reducing costs. There are several types of virtualization, including server, desktop, network, storage, and application virtualization. Virtualization relies on hypervisors, which manage resources between virtual machines. Type 1 (bare-metal) hypervisors run directly on hardware and offer better performance, while Type 2 (hosted) hypervisors run on top of a host OS. Despite its advantages—like cost efficiency, agility, and improved disaster recovery—virtualization also brings risks such as hyperjacking, VM escape, and misconfiguration vulnerabilities, which can compromise entire systems if not managed properly.

To maintain secure virtualization environments, the lesson recommends several best practices. Administrators should keep hypervisors updated, enforce strict access controls with multi-factor authentication (MFA), and follow recognized security baselines such as CIS or NIST standards. It’s also important to monitor VM behavior, control network traffic using virtual switches and VLAN segmentation, and encrypt VM images and storage. Techniques like VM isolation, resource allocation controls, and snapshot encryption further strengthen system security. Overall, the Platform Architecture lesson emphasizes that combining trusted hardware components like TPM with secure virtualization practices builds a stronger, more reliable, and well-protected computing environment.

Tips

Be Cautious with Pop-Ups and Unexpected Prompts

Avoid entering credentials or personal information into pop-up windows or unexpected login prompts. Instead, navigate directly to the official site.

Enable Two-Factor Authentication

Add an extra layer of security by enabling two-factor authentication (2FA) on your accounts whenever possible.

Monitor and Log Activities

Continuously monitor system logs and user activities to detect suspicious behavior early.

Keep Software Updated

Regularly update your operating system, applications, and security software to patch vulnerabilities.

Use Secure Connections

Always use HTTPS websites and avoid logging into sensitive accounts on public Wi-Fi without a VPN.

Backup Your Data

Keep regular backups of important files in case of malware attacks or accidental loss.

Regularly Review Account Activity

Monitor your bank, email, and other important accounts for unauthorized or suspicious activity.

Limit App Permissions

Review and restrict unnecessary permissions for apps to protect your personal data from misuse.

Log Out from Shared Devices

Always log out of accounts when using public or shared devices to prevent unauthorized access.

Use Antivirus Protection

Install and regularly update antivirus software to detect and block malware threats.

Be Careful with Downloads

Download apps and files only from trusted sources to avoid installing harmful software.

Secure Your Devices

Set up screen locks, PINs, or biometrics on your devices to prevent unauthorized physical access.
